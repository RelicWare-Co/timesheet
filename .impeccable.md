## Design Context

### Users

Colombian workers (primarily Spanish-speaking) who need to track their work hours, breaks, and overtime to verify they are being paid correctly by their employers. They use the app on mobile phones during breaks or after shifts, often in physically demanding job contexts. The primary job-to-be-done is: "Am I being paid what I'm owed?"

### Brand Personality

**Brutal, precise, advocate.** Three words: _stark, exact, protective_.

- The interface should feel like a reliable tool that leaves no room for employer manipulation.
- It is not corporate-friendly — it is worker-focused.
- Emotional goal: confidence and empowerment, not anxiety.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Neo-brutalist utility.** High-contrast black and white. No decorative gradients. No rounded corners (sharp 0px borders). Heavy uppercase display typography for headers, clean readable body text for data. The design should feel like a union pamphlet crossed with a precision instrument.

- Theme: Both light and dark modes, but always stark. Light = white paper. Dark = black ink.
- Anti-references: Glassmorphism, SaaS gradient dashboards, friendly cartoon illustration apps, corporate blue enterprise software.

### Design Principles

1. **No decorative color.** Every visual element must serve information hierarchy. Black, white, and one functional accent only (red for destructive actions).
2. **Typography is the hierarchy.** Size, weight, and case create structure — not colored backgrounds or shadows.
3. **Show value immediately.** Users should understand their pay situation within 3 seconds of opening the app. No configuration walls without context.
4. **Protect the user.** Every interaction that destroys or commits data must provide clear, reversible feedback. The app is an advocate, not a recorder.
5. **Mobile-first brutality.** Large touch targets, minimal taps, and zero hidden gestures. If it works on a cracked phone screen in a warehouse, it works everywhere.
